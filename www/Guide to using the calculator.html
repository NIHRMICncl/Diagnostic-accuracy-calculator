Guide to using the diagnostic accuracy calculator

Quick tips

  1. Enter text for titles and labels on tables and graphs
    This is useful if you want to take a screen snip to share. Meaningful labels and titles will be appreciated by your customers,  colleagues, and readers if you publish the screen snip.
    Skip this bit if you just want the calculations done.

  2. Enter information about the test
    This is quite straightforward, provided you understand the difference between the two main study designs: cohort and case-control.
    The tab "Guide to using the results of the calculator" explains why data from a cohort study cannot be used to calculate predictive values or prevalence.
    It also explains why positive and negative predictive values vary with prevalence.

  3. Use controls to tell the calculator what you want it to do:
      Draw the graphs
      The computer has to work quite hard to draw the graphs (and you will have to wait a bit), so it will only do this when you click on the "redraw graphs" button.

       Zooming in on the graph of predictive values against prevalence
      This is handy when labels overlap.
      • Move the cursor over the graph to make the controls pop up.
      • Click on the zoon button and then select the area you want enlarged.

      Get a bookmark link
      This is handy when you have entered data and labels, and want a link to save or share that can be used to restart the calculator with all the data pre-entered.
      Find this button below the "Redraw graphs" button.

Using the calculator for cohort studies

Using the calculator to compute diagnostic accuracy measures is straightforward when TP, FP, FN, and TN are available from a cohort study.

If the data available from a cohort study includes sensitivity and specificity, and you wish to compute TP, FP, FN, and TN (and positive and negative predictive values) you will also have to enter the prevalence of the condition in the target population, and the number of samples in the study.

This is a constraint of "simple" algebra: TP, FP, FN, and TN are 4 independent variables, so their computation requires 4 different independent data items.

With this knowledge, it should be straightforward to use the calculator.

Using the calculator for case-control studies

If the data available from a case-control study includes sensitivity and specificity, the calculator can be used to essentially reverse engineer the TP, FP, FN, TN counts that would have been found by a cohort study. However, the prevalence (pre-test probability) and number of samples must be provided as well as sensitivity and specificity. This allows the positive and negative predictive values to also be computed.

If the data available from a case-control study includes TP, FP, FN, and TN, you will also need to enter the prevalence and study size to enable the calculation of predictive values.
(This contrasts with a cohort study design, which allows the study size, prevalence and predictive values to be calculated from TP, FP, FN, and TN.)

The calculator essentially reverse engineers the TP, FP, FN, and TN counts that would be found in a cohort study with the same sensitivity, specificity, prevalence, and number of samples in the study.
You can see the reverse engineered TP, FP, FN, and TN counts in the 2x2 table. They will be different from the TP, FP, FN, and TN counts that you entered.

Of course, you can misuse the calculator by pretending that a case-control study was actually a cohort study, but then you would be given incorrect positive and negative predictive values. And, if you were to look at the calculated prevalence you would see that it is highly unlikely to reflect any real clinical population. Do not do this and then use the results to inform real clinical decisions!
