Worked example
To fully understand this, it might be helpful to work through an example taken from a typical evaluation of a new diagnostic test.

The Roche Elecsys Anti-SARS-CoV-2 test measures antibodies to the coronavirus that causes COVID-19, and Public Health England has published their evaluation of the test[4].
The results of Elecsys Anti-SARS-CoV-2 testing were compared in case-control studies with results from PCR testing for the virus's RNA.

The report discusses in full detail the implications of evaluating a new test for having had an infection against a test for having an active infection. The analyses took appropriate account of the fact that antibodies can take several weeks to become apparent after an infection, and that viral RNA becomes detectable a few day after infection.

For our example, we will select one subgroup: patients who were tested 7 to 13 days after their positive PCR test. The report provides the TP, FP, FN, and TN data, and gives predictive values for a prevalence of 10%. However, we would also like to know what the predictive values are at prevalence that might be found in potential populations for screening, say from 2% to 40%.

Our steps in using the calculator are:

  1. Enter titles for the tables and graphs: Roche Elecsys Anti-SARS-CoV-2 test 7-13 days post PCR confirmation.

  2. Tick the "case-control/don't know" and "TP, FP, FN, TN" boxes.
    The calculator will then require you to enter TP, FP, FN, and TN. It will also require the study size and prevalence.

  3. Enter 52, 10, 7, 5262 for TP, FP, FN, and TN, and 1000 for study size (to enable calculation of confidence intervals) and 10 for the prevalence as a percentage.

  4. Select the option to show confidence intervals.

  5. Click on Recalculate "(will take a while)"

  6. Note the 2x2 contingency matrix.
    This shows the TP, FP, FN, and TN "reverse engineered" to reflect the cohort study that would have found the same sensitivity and specificity. This is a different set of TP, FP, FN, and TN that were entered.
    This data allows predictive values to be computed and graphed to show their dependence on prevalence.

  7. Note the graph of predictive values against prevalence.
    The prevalence that was entered is shown as a vertical line with labels displaying the positive and negative predictive values at that prevalence, which is 10% in our example.

  8. Note the horizontal bar of action buttons that appears when you move the cursor over the graph.

  9. Note that the two predictive values are quite close, and this causes the labels to overlap.

  10. Click on the little zoom + button, and then click and hold while you outline a rectangle around the predictive values.
    Magically the graph will zoom in so you can see the labels separately.
