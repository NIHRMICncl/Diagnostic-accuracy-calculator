Welcome to the NIHR Newcastle MIC's calculator for the main accuracy measures of a diagnostic test

This is the first of a small collection of interactive Shiny apps that allow you to calculate diagnostic accuracy statistics and explore the intricacies of evaluating and using diagnostic tests:

  •  Diagnostic accuracy calculator.  (this app) to calculate the main diagnostic accuracy statistics from either:
    • True and false positive (TP, FP), and true and false negative (TN, FN) counts
    • Sensitivity and specificity. AND prevalence, and the size of the study from which sensitivity and specificity were estimated
  • Sample size planner. Interactive app for estimating sample sizes for diagnostic accuracy studies.
  • Interpreting diagnostic accuracy measures. Interactive app to explore the main measures of diagnostic accuracy: sensitivity and specificity (technically useful) and positive and negative predictive values (clinically useful )  (under construction).
  • Interpreting ROC curves. Interactive app to explore the trade offs between high sensitivity and high specificity when selecting the classification threshold for a test with a continuous result. (under construction).
  • Interpreting the clinical utility of diagnostic tests. Interactive app to explore how to take account of the uncertainties in test results and the values placed on the range of possible outcomes (benefits, harms, costs) when making clinical decisions. (under construction).
  • Interpreting evaluations with an imperfect reference test. cto explore the uncertainties that arise in evaluating a diagnostic test when there is no gold standard to compare its performance. (under construction).
  • Interpreting epidemic transmissibility measures. Interactive app to explore the effects of the infection's transmissibility and the test's accuracy on the course of an epidemic. (under construction).

"Technical accuracy" of a diagnostic test

We use the term technical accuracy (which answers questions about the accuracy of classification) to contrast with clinical accuracy (which answers questions about the accuracy of prediction given that the test result is positive or negative).

The accuracy of a new diagnostic test is assessed by comparing results from the new test with those from a reference test.
When the tests agree, the new test's results are true positive (TP) or true negative (TN).
When the tests do not agree, the new test's results are false positive (FP) or false negative (FN).

Sensitivity is the true positive rate: TP / (TP + FN). Sensitivity is measured in people who have the condition being tested for.
Specificity is the true negative rate: TN  (TN + FP). Specificity is measured in people who do not have the condition being tested for.

The accuracy of a diagnostic test can be assessed in either a case-control study or a cohort study.

A case-control study has two groups of participants: people who are known to have the disease (to estimate sensitivity), and people who are known to not have the condition (to estimate specificity).

A cohort study has one group of participants: people who may or may not have the condition, and therefore sensitivity and specificity can be calculated.

If the cohort is representative of the population in which the test will be used, the relevant prevalence of the condition can be calculated (as TP / (TP + FP + FN + TN), and this allows the relevant positive and negative predictive values to be calculated.

Prevalence should never be calculated from a case-control study, because it will not apply to a real population.

Since predictive values depend on prevalence, positive and negative predictive values calculated from a case-control study will be misleading, wrong, erroneous, incorrect, invalid, false, and untrue,
